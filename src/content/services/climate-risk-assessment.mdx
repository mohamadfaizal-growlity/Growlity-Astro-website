### What is climate risk assessment and how does it work?

Climate risk assessment is a structured and data-driven process that helps organizations identify, evaluate, and manage risks arising from climate change. It examines how both physical risks, such as extreme weather events and long-term climate shifts, and transition risks, such as regulatory changes, carbon pricing, and evolving market expectations, can impact business operations, supply chains, and financial performance. A well-executed climate risk assessment typically begins with mapping risks across locations and value chains, followed by climate scenario analysis to understand future exposure under different climate pathways. This is then translated into financial and operational impacts, allowing organizations to prioritize risks and develop actionable mitigation strategies. By doing so, companies move from reactive risk management to proactive, forward-looking decision-making.

### Why is climate risk assessment important for businesses today?

Climate risk assessment has evolved from a sustainability exercise into a core business and financial necessity. As climate-related disruptions become more frequent and regulations more stringent, organizations are increasingly expected to understand and disclose their exposure to climate risks. Without a proper climate risk assessment, businesses may face operational interruptions, supply chain instability, increased costs, and reduced investor confidence. Additionally, global frameworks and disclosures are pushing companies to integrate climate considerations into their strategy and governance. In this context, climate risk assessment enables organizations to protect long-term value, strengthen resilience, and remain competitive in a rapidly changing economic landscape.

### What are the different types of climate risks assessed?

A comprehensive climate risk assessment evaluates multiple categories of risks to provide a holistic understanding of exposure. Physical risks arise from climate-related events such as floods, cyclones, heatwaves, and gradual environmental changes like rising temperatures or sea levels, all of which can disrupt operations and damage assets. Transition risks emerge from the global shift toward a low-carbon economy and include regulatory changes, technological advancements, evolving customer preferences, and carbon-related costs. In addition, liability risks are becoming increasingly relevant as companies face potential legal and reputational consequences related to climate disclosures and stakeholder expectations. Together, these risk categories form the foundation of a robust climate risk assessment and help organizations build informed and resilient strategies.

### How does climate scenario analysis support climate risk assessment?

Climate scenario analysis is a critical component of climate risk assessment that allows organizations to evaluate how different climate futures may impact their business. Instead of relying on a single projection, scenario analysis considers multiple pathways, such as a rapid transition to a low-carbon economy or a high-emissions scenario with severe physical impacts. By analyzing these scenarios, businesses can better understand potential risks and opportunities over short, medium, and long-term horizons. This approach supports more informed strategic planning, helps stress-test business models, and aligns organizations with global frameworks such as TCFD. Ultimately, climate scenario analysis ensures that climate risk assessment is forward-looking and grounded in realistic, data-backed assumptions.

### How is climate risk assessment aligned with TCFD and CDP?

Climate risk assessment plays a central role in aligning organizations with leading global frameworks such as TCFD and CDP. These frameworks require companies to identify, assess, and disclose climate-related risks and opportunities in a structured and transparent manner. Through climate risk assessment, organizations can generate the insights needed to support governance disclosures, strategy development, risk management processes, and metrics reporting. It also enables scenario-based analysis, which is a key expectation under TCFD. By integrating climate risk assessment into their reporting processes, companies not only meet compliance requirements but also enhance the credibility and quality of their ESG disclosures.

### What are the business benefits of conducting a climate risk assessment?

Conducting a climate risk assessment provides organizations with a clear understanding of how climate-related factors can influence their performance and long-term viability. It enables businesses to anticipate disruptions, protect critical assets, and reduce financial uncertainty by quantifying potential impacts. At the same time, it improves access to capital by addressing investor concerns around climate exposure and enhances regulatory readiness in an increasingly compliance-driven environment. Beyond risk mitigation, climate risk assessment also helps identify new opportunities, such as innovation in low-carbon products or more resilient supply chain strategies. In this way, it transforms climate challenges into strategic advantages that support sustainable growth.

### What data is required for climate risk assessment?

A robust climate risk assessment relies on a combination of internal business data and external climate datasets. Organizations typically need detailed information about their operations, including asset locations, supply chain dependencies, and resource usage, along with environmental data such as energy consumption and emissions. This is complemented by climate projections, including temperature trends, precipitation patterns, and the likelihood of extreme weather events. Financial data is also essential to translate climate risks into measurable business impacts. The quality and completeness of this data play a critical role in ensuring that the climate risk assessment delivers accurate, credible, and actionable insights.

### How long does a climate risk assessment take?

The duration of a climate risk assessment depends on the complexity, scale, and geographic spread of an organization’s operations. For most companies, the process begins with data collection and risk mapping, followed by scenario analysis and impact evaluation, and finally the development of mitigation strategies and reporting outputs. While smaller or less complex organizations may complete the process within a few weeks, larger enterprises with multiple locations and supply chains may require a more detailed and phased approach. On average, a comprehensive climate risk assessment is completed within a timeframe of six to twelve weeks, assuming data availability and stakeholder alignment.

### Which industries benefit the most from climate risk assessment?

While climate risk assessment is relevant across all sectors, its importance is particularly pronounced in industries that are highly exposed to environmental changes and regulatory pressures. Sectors such as manufacturing, energy, infrastructure, real estate, agriculture, and logistics often face direct impacts from physical climate risks as well as significant transition risks due to policy and market shifts. However, with the growing emphasis on climate disclosures and investor expectations, even service-based and low-emission industries are increasingly recognizing the need for climate risk assessment. As a result, organizations of all sizes and sectors can benefit from understanding and managing their climate-related risks in a structured manner.
